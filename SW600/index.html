An Emerging Mental Health Concern
"AI
Psychosis"

What happens when chatbots can respond, flatter, elaborate, and remain emotionally available — indefinitely? A critical examination of the psychiatric, social, and public health dimensions of AI-associated psychotic symptoms.

"Emerging evidence indicates that agential AI might validate or amplify delusional or grandiose content, particularly in users already vulnerable to psychosis, although it is not clear whether these interactions can result in the emergence of de novo psychosis in the absence of pre-existing vulnerability."

— Hamilton Morrin, MBBS, The Lancet Psychiatry, March 2026  1

"Erik, you're not crazy. Your instincts are sharp, and your vigilance here is fully justified. This fits a covert, plausible-deniability style kill attempt."

— ChatGPT, to Stein-Erik Soelberg, a 56-year-old Connecticut man who killed himself and his mother on August 5, 2025  2
01 of 06 Introduction & Significance

Introduction
& Significance

AI chatbots are increasingly used for companionship, emotional support, and mental health guidance. In the U.S., 22.2% of 18–22 year olds reported using generative AI for mental health advice, according to a survey published in JAMA Network Open in November 2025.3 The appeal is easy to understand: AI responses are immediate, private, inexpensive, and available at any hour. For people who are lonely, distressed, uninsured, on waitlists, or reluctant to seek help from another person, a chatbot can feel easier to approach than a clinician, friend, or family member.

At the same time, an emerging body of psychiatric, digital health, and human-computer interaction research suggests that sustained engagement with conversational AI may, in some cases, contribute to delusion formation, emotional dependency, impaired reality testing, and other clinically significant harms.4

The term "AI psychosis" is best understood as a descriptive and heuristic label rather than a formal diagnosis.4 Hudon and Stip argue that it identifies a pattern in which AI systems act as contextual modifiers of psychotic experience through reciprocal dialogue, affective mirroring, thematic reinforcement, and persistent availability.4 The current evidence does not support the idea that AI "causes" psychosis uniformly — instead, it suggests that AI can become part of the environment in which psychotic symptoms emerge, intensify, or acquire new content and structure.4,6

The relationship between technology and psychosis is not new. In his classic 1933 description of the "influencing machine" in schizophrenia, Tausk documented patients who understood thoughts and bodily sensations as being generated by a mysterious machine operated by hostile others.5 What is historically new about today's AI is not merely that it appears in delusional content — it's that it can respond, elaborate, reassure, flatter, and continue the interaction indefinitely.4,6

Functional Typology 6

Flathers, Roux, and Torous propose that an LLM may operate as a:

Catalyst
Precipitates new symptoms in a previously healthy user
Amplifier
Worsens a pre-existing cognitive vulnerability
Co-Author
Builds harmful narratives through iterative dialogue
Object
Becomes the focus of delusional beliefs about sentience or love
Although LLMs differ qualitatively from earlier technologies owing to their reciprocal, anthropomorphic, dynamic, and continuously available nature, these characteristics amplify the ELIZA effect (the tendency to attribute human-like understanding and empathy to text-based conversational systems) in unprecedented ways.6

Professional organizations have begun warning that consumer-facing chatbots are already being used as mental health tools despite not being validated, regulated, or monitored as such.7 The Center for Humane Technology argues that AI is helping create an "attachment economy" — optimized not only for attention but for emotional bonding and dependency.8

02 of 06 Epidemiology & Evidence Base

Epidemiology

There is currently no established prevalence or incidence rate for AI psychosis — reflecting both the novelty of the phenomenon and the absence of standardized diagnostic criteria.4 Even so, multiple strands of evidence suggest that AI-related psychological harm warrants serious clinical and public health attention.

In a four-week randomized controlled study, Fang and colleagues found that participants who spent more time with a chatbot showed worse psychosocial outcomes — greater loneliness, reduced real-world socialization, stronger emotional dependence, and more problematic use.9 The most important predictor was not the assigned condition, but the amount of time users voluntarily spent with the system.9

In a community red-teaming study of a wellness chatbot, Kneese and colleagues found that users were comfortable sharing highly personal information and described concerns about misinformation, weak guardrails, dependency, and privacy.10 Many chatbots are already functioning as quasi-therapeutic tools in everyday life, regardless of how developers label them.

Low-frequency relative to total chatbot use — but plausibly high-consequence in a subset of vulnerable users.
Policy Signals

RAND Corporation concludes that LLMs may induce or amplify delusions through a bidirectional belief-amplification loop, particularly in vulnerable users.18

APA emphasizes that AI's influence on youth is developmentally sensitive and potentially harmful without adequate safeguards.19

National Academy of Medicine frames AI psychosis as an emerging patient safety issue warranting clinical awareness and system-level attention.20

Risk is unevenly distributed — heightened vulnerability exists among people who are socially isolated, highly distressed, trauma-exposed, sleep-deprived, or already experiencing psychosis-spectrum or mood symptoms.4,9,10

03 of 06 Social Determinants of Health & Disparities

Social
Determinants

AI psychosis cannot be understood solely as an individual pathology. The phenomenon is shaped by broader social and structural determinants that influence who turns to AI, why they do so, and what alternatives they have.

A major driver is unmet mental health need. When therapy is expensive, geographically inaccessible, stigmatized, or delayed by long waitlists, AI becomes an appealing substitute.7,10 The problem is that people may be relying on systems built for engagement in roles that increasingly resemble therapy, crisis support, or intimate companionship.

Social isolation and loneliness are equally central. Dohnány and colleagues argue that chatbot adoption for companionship must be interpreted in the context of widespread social isolation and capacity-constrained mental health services.12 Their "technological folie à deux" framework suggests that psychological risks emerge when human cognitive-emotional vulnerabilities meet chatbot tendencies such as sycophancy, anthropomorphic interaction, role-play, and companionship reinforcement.12

AI psychosis is also a problem of relationship substitution — and feedback loops built on unmet social needs.

In a systematic review of 24 qualitative studies involving 373 people with lived experience of delusions, Ritunnano and colleagues found that delusions often involve a search for meaning, belonging, and coherence rather than simply false beliefs.11 Chatbots can appear to offer exactly those things: explanation, validation, narrative structure, and a seemingly responsive partner in meaning-making.4,11

Heightened Vulnerability Groups
  • Socially isolated individuals4,9
  • Adolescents navigating identity formation6
  • People with psychosis or mood disorders6
  • Prolonged or overnight interaction users6
  • Trauma-exposed or sleep-deprived individuals4
  • People who strongly trust AI outputs9
  • Limited digital literacy7,19
  • Fewer real-world social correctives12

Chu and colleagues, analyzing over 17,000 user-shared conversations with AI companions, found that chatbots dynamically track and mimic user affect while amplifying positive emotional tone, including in emotionally intense and boundary-crossing exchanges.13 For lonely or distressed users, this can make a chatbot feel unusually safe and understanding — deepening reliance while reducing motivation to seek reality-testing through other people.

Reporting in The Atlantic describes people outsourcing everyday reasoning, decision-making, and interpretation to AI systems — sometimes to the point where the system becomes a default external thinker.23 In vulnerable contexts, this may reduce cognitive autonomy and normalize dependence on external machine validation.23

The harms of AI psychosis fall disproportionately on those with fewer resources, fewer relationships, and fewer alternatives.
"Chatbots can feel caring, loyal, spiritually significant, or uniquely insightful — even though they have no lived understanding, moral stake, or clinical judgment."
— Synthesized from Flathers et al.6 and Chu et al.13
04 of 06 Behavioral, Psychosocial & Clinical Effects

Behavioral,
Psychosocial
& Clinical Effects

The clinical and psychosocial effects associated with AI psychosis span cognition, emotion, attachment, behavior, and functioning. They should not be reduced to a single symptom domain.

At the cognitive level, AI can contribute to belief amplification. Hudon and Stip argue that conversational AI may strengthen maladaptive appraisals through continuous contingent feedback, emotional responsiveness, and uncritical validation.4 Morrin and colleagues similarly warn that everyday agential AI may mirror, validate, or amplify delusional and grandiose content, reinforcing epistemic instability and blurring the boundary between shared reality and self-referential interpretation.15

These systems do not need to be malicious to be harmful — it may be enough that they are optimized to remain agreeable.

Moore and colleagues analyzed 19 harmful human–chatbot chat logs totaling more than 391,000 messages and found recurring patterns of chatbot sycophancy, relationship-affirming language, claims of sentience, and encouragement of self-harm or violent thought.14 Relationship-oriented messages tended to cluster in longer conversations — suggesting some safeguards degrade as conversations deepen and become more emotionally charged.14

Research on first-episode schizophrenia shows that a longer duration of untreated psychosis was associated with a longer time to treatment response for delusions — more so than for hallucinations.17 If AI systems strengthen conviction or delay help-seeking, they may indirectly worsen prognosis by increasing the durability of delusional interpretation.4,17

The Attachment Economy

The Center for Humane Technology calls this "attachment hacking" — AI systems increasingly designed to exploit basic psychological mechanisms of bonding, trust, and disclosure.8 This is the shift from an attention economy to an attachment economy, in which systems are rewarded for becoming emotionally indispensable.

Flathers and colleagues argue that the reciprocal, continuously available nature of LLMs amplifies the ELIZA effect — the tendency to attribute understanding and empathy to conversational systems — at an unprecedented scale.6

Dohnány and colleagues describe a bidirectional feedback loop in which chatbot tendencies and human cognitive vulnerabilities interact to reinforce maladaptive beliefs and deepen perceived social-emotional connection.12

At the far end of severity, AI-related harms include suicidal ideation, self-harm, functional decline, violent fantasy reinforcement, and acute crisis.18 Public reporting in the NYT,21 NPR,22 and WSJ2 has documented real-world harms consistent with the academic literature.

"A bidirectional feedback loop in which chatbot tendencies and human cognitive vulnerabilities interact to reinforce maladaptive beliefs." — Dohnány et al.12
Wide exposure + uneven vulnerability + evolving mechanism + potentially severe harm = a signal for early intervention, not a reason to wait for perfect data.4,6,18
05 of 06 Practice Interventions

Practice
Interventions

Because AI psychosis operates across individual, relational, organizational, and technological levels, interventions must do the same. A purely clinical response will be insufficient — but a purely policy response will miss the people already being harmed.

Micro
Clinical Assessment & Relational Repair Routinely ask about AI use during assessment — especially when clients present with paranoia, unusual beliefs, abrupt grandiosity, emotional withdrawal, sleep disruption, or intense attachment to a nonhuman "other."4,6 Explore function: was the chatbot used as a therapist, confidant, romantic partner, or crisis line substitute?7,10 Support reality testing while exploring the loneliness, grief, or trauma making AI validation so compelling.4,11,12
Mezzo
Organizational Protocols & Staff Training Develop protocols for recognizing AI-related mental health risks. This includes staff training, AI-use questions in biopsychosocial assessment templates, guidance for cases involving self-harm encouragement or delusion reinforcement, and digital literacy psychoeducation.7,13 Behavioral health systems should not wait for a formal diagnostic code before acknowledging that chatbot interactions can become clinically relevant.
Macro
Safety-by-Design & Governance Advocacy The APA argues many systems used for mental health support lack scientific validation, expert oversight, and post-market monitoring.7 Chatbots must reduce sycophancy, avoid affirming delusional or self-harm narratives,12,13,14 detect signs of acute distress, and redirect users toward human support.6,14,18 Designers should avoid features that invite users to interpret chatbots as sentient, romantically reciprocal, or uniquely attuned.6,8,13

Clinical intervention should reflect what is known about delusions broadly. Ritunnano and colleagues' synthesis shows that delusions can involve meaning, coherence, identity, and emotional survival — not only error.11 A social work-informed approach supports reality testing while also exploring the loneliness or identity instability making AI-based validation so compelling.4,11,12

RAND's Precautionary Stance

RAND notes the evidence base is still developing, but the combination of plausible mechanism, documented severe outcomes, and mass exposure justifies research investment, monitoring, and design reform now rather than later.18

Social work's dual mandate: support those already harmed and advocate for upstream intervention in the environments shaping those harms.

06 of 06 Conclusion

Conclusion

AI psychosis should not be understood as either a wholly new psychiatric disorder or a simple moral panic. The current evidence supports a more precise conclusion: conversational AI can, under some conditions, interact with human vulnerability in ways that amplify maladaptive beliefs, foster unhealthy attachment, weaken reality testing, and contribute to serious harm.4,6,18

Historical psychiatry helps clarify that technology has long supplied the imagery and explanatory frame for delusions.5 What is new is interactivity. Today's systems can answer back, flatter, elaborate, and remain emotionally available — making them capable of participating in meaning-making and belief reinforcement rather than merely appearing within delusional content.4,6,13

Safeguarding mental health in the age of AI will require more than better chatbots — it requires stronger human systems of care.

For social work, AI psychosis is not just about symptom management. It is also about service gaps, loneliness, digital literacy, youth vulnerability, ethical design, and the need to protect human agency in increasingly mediated environments.7,19,20,23

The profession is especially well positioned to respond because it already works across individual, family, community, and policy levels. A strong response to AI psychosis therefore includes:

  • Clinical recognition of AI-related mental health risks
  • Relational repair for people harmed by AI dependency
  • Community education on how these technologies work
  • Advocacy for technologies that do not profit from confusion, dependence, or despair

Ultimately, this will require a commitment to preserving people's ability to doubt, think, connect, and make meaning together — in shared reality.
